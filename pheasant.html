The male ring-necked pheasant is easy to spot, with its showy iridescent plumage and rooster-like call. They are common to Calgary’s urban parks, especially Fish Creek Provincial Park. They live in southern Alberta year-round, preferring grasslands, river valleys and coulees. Here are five things about this fascinating bird.

They’re not from around here
Ring-necked pheasants were actually introduced to North America from China and Japan in the late 1880s. They arrived in the Strathmore area in 1908, where the population built up slowly. By the 1930s, they were well-established in the Brooks area. Globally, it’s one of the most hunted birds. They are bred to be hunted and are shot in great numbers in Europe. Pheasant farming is popular, especially in the U.K.
Moms ‘speak’ to their chicks
Pheasant hens communicate with their chicks even before they hatch. The hens chirp to their eggs while incubating. This allows the chicks to recognize their mother’s voice as soon as they hatch and it trains them to respond to her calls when they leave the nest. Generally, they leave the nest three weeks after hatching.
Thanks to a healthy store of fat, the pheasant is able to ride out a blizzard for up to three days. Still, the biggest cause of winter mortality is freezing, when the bird loses more heat than it can produce. This is why good winter habitat is essential to the bird's survival. The pheasant finds cover under dense vegetation, which also provides sanctuary from coyotes and birds of prey.

They’re good breeders
In optimal conditions, a single hen may lay more than 30 eggs in a breeding season. Many of those eggs are laid before she has even built a nest. Once a nest is built, the hen will lay one egg a day until it’s full with 10 or 12. The hen then starts incubating, spending up to 23 hours a day on the nest, taking short breaks to feed. The eggs usually hatch after 23 days.
The Alberta Conservation Program will release 26,000 pheasants over 41 sites this year. It’s the third year of the release program, which is intended to revive pheasant hunting in Alberta — once a prime hunting destination for the bird.
